How RallyRank works

Plain English. No jargon. With a calculator at the bottom you can poke at.

Reading time: about 90 seconds. Skip to the calculator if you just want to play.

The big idea

We don't just count wins. After every match, the system updates both a guess of how good you are and how confident it is in that guess. Two numbers per player, refined every time you step on court.

Two numbers: μ (skill) and σ (uncertainty)

Every player has two numbers behind their name:

  • μ ("mu") — the system's best guess of your skill. Higher = stronger.
  • σ ("sigma") — how unsure the system is. Higher = less confident. Shrinks as you play more matches.

You can picture each player as a bell curve: μ is the peak, σ is the width. The wider the curve, the less the system trusts the number yet.

Avery has played a lot — narrow bell, system is confident. Blake is new — wide bell, system is hedging until they play more.

Why the leaderboard sorts by μ − 3σ

If we ranked by μ alone, a brand-new player who happened to win their first match would shoot to the top. So we use a conservative number: μ − 3σ. It's roughly "the lowest skill we're 99% sure this player is at least as strong as."

Practical effect: new players start near the bottom and climb as their σ shrinks. Once you've played ~15+ matches, σ has shrunk enough that μ − 3σ is close to your real skill estimate.

What changes a rating after a match

Win or lose, both teams' ratings move. The base move is whatever OpenSkill's math says (bigger upset ⇒ bigger move). On top of that, four multipliers stack to scale the change up or down:

  • Game volume — a 26-game battle is more informative than a 6-0 6-0 blowout, so it changes ratings more.
  • Division K — a Div 1 win moves your rating more than a Div 4 win (higher division = stronger field = stronger signal).
  • Time decay — matches from two years ago count for less than matches from last month. Recent form weighs heavier.
  • Partner weighting — within a pair, the stronger partner's rating moves a bit more (they probably contributed more). Net team change is preserved.

Walkovers count, but at half weight — the win was real, the play wasn't.

Two leaderboards: KC Version vs DF Version

The leaderboard has a tab toggle between KC Version and DF Version. Both rank the same 696 players using the same 4,062 matches — the source data is identical. What differs is the algorithm reading those matches.

If you don't do maths

Think of it like two referees watching the same game and writing down "skill marks" for each player. They see the same plays, but they keep score on different sheets:

  • KC's referee (OpenSkill) uses a 0-to-50ish scale. After each match they nudge the four players' marks up or down a tiny amount based on whether the result was expected (small nudge) or surprising (big nudge), and how lopsided the games were.
  • DF's referee (Glicko-2) uses a 0-to-3000ish scale (chess uses the same scale). They do the same idea — nudge marks up or down — but the maths underneath is shaped differently. Each player carries a "reliability" number that shrinks the more they play.

Both referees agree on the question — "given everyone's history, how should this match shift things?" — and both arrive at roughly the same ranking. But the maths they use is different, so the answers won't be identical. That's the point.

So why have both? A rating system is a model of reality, and every model has blind spots. Running two side-by-side is a built-in BS detector. If KC says you're player #5 and DF says you're player #50, that's a flag worth a closer look — usually a small data quirk (very few matches, weird tournament format) that one model handles better than the other. Where they agree, you can trust the answer. Where they disagree, dig in.

If you do maths — the technical comparison

Both algorithms are Bayesian rating systems: each player has a prior distribution over their "true skill", and every match is a likelihood observation that updates the posterior. They differ in which prior, which likelihood, and how doubles is handled.

Shared scaffolding

  • Score function. Both consume S = games_won / (games_won + games_lost) ∈ [0, 1] — the "universal score" from PLAN.md §5.2. Replaces the cruder binary win/loss with a continuous outcome that captures match dominance. Walkovers are clamped to S = 0.90 / 0.10 (the win was real; the play wasn't).
  • Conservative rank. Both display "μ − 3σ" (KC) or "r − 3·RD" (DF) — the lower 99% bound of the player's posterior. New players sit near the floor and rise as uncertainty shrinks.
  • Source iterator. Both call rating._iter_active_matches(conn) in chronological order. Same 4,062 matches, same filter (superseded_by_run_id IS NULL), same order.

KC — OpenSkill Plackett-Luce

  • Prior: each player has skill θ ~ 𝒩(μ, σ²). Default μ = 25, σ = 25/3.
  • Likelihood: a Plackett-Luce ranking model — P(team A finishes ahead of team B) ∝ exp(strengthA) / (exp(strengthA) + exp(strengthB)) — where each team's strength is derived from its players' θ.
  • Update: approximate Bayesian inference produces closed-form (μ, σ) updates per player after each match. Bigger upset = larger μ shift. Higher prior σ = larger update step.
  • Doubles: native multi-agent — Plackett-Luce treats each team as the sum of its players' skills, and the per-player update is split fairly across the team.
  • On top of OpenSkill's base move, the production engine layers four multipliers: game volume, division K, time decay (τ = 365 d), partner-share weighting. See "What changes a rating" above.

DF — Glicko-2 (doubles-adapted)

  • Prior: each player has rating r ~ 𝒩(r̂, RD²) and a volatility σ tracking rating reliability over time. Default r = 1500, RD = 350, σ = 0.06.
  • Likelihood: logistic — E[score against opponent] = 1 / (1 + exp(−g(RDopp)·(r − ropp) / 400·ln 10)), where g(RD) = 1 / √(1 + 3·RD² / π²) dampens the update for opponents with high uncertainty.
  • Update: Glickman's 2012 paper — closed-form updates to (r, RD, σ) per match. r shifts toward the observed outcome scaled by g(RDopp); RD shrinks; σ adjusts based on whether recent results were surprising.
  • Doubles: the opponent team is collapsed to a single virtual entity — rteam = average of partner r's, RDteam = √((RDp1² + RDp2²) / 2) (RMS-combined). The focal player updates against this virtual opponent. Approximation, not native — but well-behaved in practice.
  • Time handling: RD inflates between rating periods (volatility-driven), no separate decay multiplier.

Where they will disagree

  • Players with very few matches. KC's μ−3σ pushes new players toward the floor more aggressively than DF's r−3·RD; rapid risers may rank lower under KC for longer.
  • Players with long inactive gaps. KC has explicit time-decay multiplier τ = 365 d (older matches contribute less). DF's volatility-driven RD inflation grows uncertainty during gaps but doesn't down-weight old results — so a comeback player's rank can lag in DF until a few new matches recalibrate.
  • Lopsided pairs. KC's partner-share multiplier credits the stronger partner with more of the team's update. DF's symmetric doubles aggregation splits credit equally. A clearly dominant player paired with a weaker partner may rank slightly higher under KC.
  • Strong wins by underdogs. Plackett-Luce's exponential and Glicko-2's logistic disagree most sharply at the tails — surprise upsets shift ratings differently in the two models.

If a specific player ranks 50+ places apart between the two leaderboards, that's the most useful signal the dual-model architecture produces. It usually points at one of the divergence cases above — worth a closer look at their match history before trusting either rank as gospel.

Captain's say sits on top

For tournaments where captains pre-assign players to A/B/C/D teams, the leaderboard groups by that captain choice first, then ranks by μ − 3σ within the group. Math knows numbers; captains know things math can't see (chemistry, injury, attitude). Both views are visible — the default respects captains, and a "raw" sort shows pure math.
